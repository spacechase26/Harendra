## TL;DR — the verdict

Pidilite is a genuinely **wonderful business** — a near-monopoly brand (Fevicol), 31%
ROCE, ~24% operating margins, almost no debt. But on a base-case discounted-cash-flow
model it's worth roughly **₹560/share** (range **₹410–₹910** across reasonable
assumptions), versus a market price of **₹1,455**.

So the business is not the question — the **price** is. At ₹1,455 the market is paying
~60× earnings and effectively pricing in **~10% growth in perpetuity**. That's a bet on
decades of continued dominance. Two honest readings sit side by side:

- **On cash-flow math, it's expensive** — little margin of safety; a disciplined buyer waits for a pullback.
- **Against its quality peer set, it's not an outlier** — Asian Paints trades ~66×, Titan ~86×. The market pays this premium for *all* the great Indian consumer franchises.

**Bottom line:** a high-quality compounder at a demanding price. I'd want it on a watch-list
and buy on weakness, not at 60× with no margin of safety.

## 1. The business (one paragraph)

Pidilite makes adhesives, sealants and construction chemicals. You already know its
products: **Fevicol, M-Seal, Fevikwik, Dr. Fixit, Fevicryl**. The moat is brand + distribution:
"Fevicol" is the *generic word* for adhesive to a carpenter, sold through a vast network of
hardware retailers. That pricing power shows up in the numbers — high margins, high return
on capital, and the ability to pass on raw-material (VAM, crude-linked) cost swings over time.
It's a simple, cash-generative, low-debt business — which is exactly why it's a clean DCF case.

## 3. How I valued it

Two lenses, which is standard practice:
1. **DCF (intrinsic)** — project free cash flow to the firm (FCFF), discount it. This is the "what is it worth on its own cash" view.
2. **Relative (multiples)** — compare its P/E to comparable quality consumer franchises.

DCF is the anchor; relative is the sanity-check.

## 4. The DCF

**Assumptions (these are the judgment calls — own them):**

| Assumption | Value | Reasoning (be ready to defend) |
|---|---|---|
| Explicit forecast horizon | 5 years (FY27–FY31) | Long enough to capture the growth fade. |
| Revenue growth | 12% → 10% (fading) | Below the 5-yr ~10–15% history; a large base grows slower. |
| EBIT margin | 22% | EBITDA ~24% less D&A ~2.5% of sales. *Verify the D&A bridge.* |
| Tax rate | 25% | Indian new-regime corporate rate (~25.17%). |
| Capex | 3% of sales | Asset-light; slightly above D&A for growth. *Verify vs cash-flow statement.* |
| D&A | 2.5% of sales | *Verify vs cash-flow statement.* |
| Change in working capital | 8% of incremental revenue | Consumer business with inventory + receivables. *Verify the WC cycle.* |
| WACC | 11.5% | CAPM: Rf ~6.8% (10-yr G-sec) + β ~0.8 × ERP ~6%. Near-zero debt → ~all-equity. |
| Terminal growth (g) | 6.5% | Below India's ~7–8% nominal GDP; a durable franchise, but no perpetual outperformance assumed. |
| Net debt | ~0 | Net cash; conservatively treated as zero. *Verify cash balance.* |
| Shares outstanding | 101.8 Cr | Derived from market cap ÷ price. *Confirm.* |

**Projection & discounting (₹ Crore):**

| Line | FY27 | FY28 | FY29 | FY30 | FY31 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue | 16,353 | 18,152 | 20,148 | 22,163 | 24,379 |
| EBIT (22%) | 3,598 | 3,993 | 4,433 | 4,876 | 5,363 |
| NOPAT = EBIT×(1−25%) | 2,698 | 2,995 | 3,325 | 3,657 | 4,022 |
| + D&A (2.5%) | 409 | 454 | 504 | 554 | 609 |
| − Capex (3%) | 491 | 545 | 604 | 665 | 731 |
| − Δ Working capital | 140 | 144 | 160 | 161 | 177 |
| **FCFF** | **2,476** | **2,760** | **3,065** | **3,385** | **3,723** |
| Discount factor @ 11.5% | 0.897 | 0.804 | 0.721 | 0.647 | 0.580 |
| **PV of FCFF** | **2,221** | **2,220** | **2,211** | **2,190** | **2,160** |

**Bringing it together:**

- Sum of PV (explicit 5 yrs) = **₹11,002 Cr**
- Terminal value = FCFF₃₁ × (1+g) ÷ (WACC − g) = 3,723 × 1.065 ÷ (0.115 − 0.065) = **₹79,300 Cr**
- PV of terminal value = 79,300 × 0.580 = **₹46,019 Cr**
- **Enterprise value = 11,002 + 46,019 = ₹57,021 Cr**
- Less net debt (~0) → **Equity value ≈ ₹57,021 Cr**
- ÷ 101.8 Cr shares → **Intrinsic value ≈ ₹560/share**

vs market **₹1,455** → the base case implies the stock is **~60% above** my intrinsic value.
(Note this also implies a "fair" P/E of ~23× — rich for most companies, but the market clearly
pays far more for franchises of this quality.)

## 5. Sensitivity (intrinsic ₹/share)

The terminal value does the heavy lifting, so the answer is most sensitive to WACC and g:

| WACC ↓ / g → | g = 5.5% | g = 6.5% | g = 7.5% |
|---|---|---|---|
| **10.5%** | 579 | **702** | 907 |
| **11.5%** | 481 | **560** | 678 |
| **12.5%** | 411 | **465** | 542 |

Even the most generous corner (₹907) sits well below ₹1,455. The valuation gap is robust to
the assumptions — it doesn't hinge on one pessimistic input.

## 6. Reverse DCF — what does ₹1,455 require you to believe?

Flip the model: hold WACC at 11.5% and solve for the terminal growth that *justifies* the price.
To reach an equity value of ₹1,48,000 Cr, you need **g ≈ 9.8%** — i.e. the market is pricing
Pidilite to grow its cash flows at nearly **10% forever** (or, equivalently, applying a much lower
discount rate because it sees the business as nearly risk-free). That's the bull case in one number:
*durable ~10% compounding for decades.* Plausible for a franchise this strong — but it's an
optimistic bar, and it's what you're paying for today.

The tension worth articulating: **on absolute cash-flow math Pidilite is expensive, but
relative to its quality peer set its 60× is middle-of-the-pack.** Both statements are true.
That's the mark of a thoughtful note — you hold both, rather than declaring a simple "buy" or "sell."

## 8. Risks / what would change my mind

- **Multiple re-rating:** if premium-consumer P/Es compress (rising rates, a risk-off market), Pidilite's price could fall toward intrinsic value regardless of business performance.
- **Input costs:** VAM / crude-linked raw materials — a sustained spike compresses margins before price hikes catch up.
- **Competition / private label** in construction chemicals and adhesives.
- **Growth disappoints:** the price needs ~10% perpetual growth; any structural slowdown re-rates it hard.
- **On the other side:** great compounders have looked "expensive" for years and kept compounding — so a too-low intrinsic estimate (too-high WACC / too-low g) is also a real risk. This is about *entry discipline*, not business quality.
