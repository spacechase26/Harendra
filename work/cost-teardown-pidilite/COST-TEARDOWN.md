## TL;DR — the headline

Out of every **₹100** Pidilite earns in revenue, roughly **₹47 is raw materials**, **₹24 is
operating profit**, and the rest is people and overheads. The whole story of the last four
years is in that materials number: as input costs (VAM / crude-linked chemicals) **spiked then
normalised**, Pidilite's operating margin swung from **17% (FY23) to 24% (FY26)** — a ~700 bps
jump — *without needing huge revenue growth.* It's a high-contribution-margin business
(~48%) that breaks even at only ~half its current sales, which is exactly why a brand like
Fevicol is such a powerful profit engine.

This is the cleanest honest "unit economics" you can build from public data — decompose

only the split of the 76 of cost is estimated.

So ~₹0.47 of every rupee is bought-in materials, and ~₹0.17 drops to the bottom line — a
genuinely high net margin for a manufacturer, and the signature of pricing power.

## 3. The margin story — why operating margin went 17% → 24%

This is the insight that makes the teardown worth reading.

| Fiscal year (Mar) | FY22 | FY23 | FY24 | FY25 | FY26 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue (₹ Cr) | 9,921 | 11,799 | 12,383 | 13,140 | 14,601 |
| **Operating margin** | **19%** | **17%** | **22%** | **23%** | **24%** |

Revenue grew steadily, but margin **dipped to 17% in FY23 and then climbed 700 bps to 24%.**
A revenue story can't explain a dip-then-recovery like that — a **cost** story can:

- Pidilite's biggest input is **VAM (vinyl acetate monomer)** and other crude-linked chemicals.
- In FY22–FY23, those prices **spiked**, pushing raw-material cost as a % of sales up (squeezing
  margins to 17%).
- From FY24, input prices **normalised**, so raw-material % fell — and because Pidilite **held
  its selling prices** (brand pricing power), the saving dropped almost entirely to margin.

**Takeaway:** Pidilite's profitability is, in the short run, a *leveraged play on the input-cost
cycle* — but its brand lets it keep the gains rather than compete them away. *(Verify the
raw-material-cost % trend against FY22–FY26 annual reports to put exact numbers on this.)*

## 4. Fixed vs variable → contribution & break-even

Reclassifying the cost base (illustrative split — label your assumptions):

- **Variable cost** ≈ raw materials + variable freight/power ≈ **~52% of revenue**
- **Contribution margin** ≈ **48%**
- **Fixed costs** (employees, advertising, fixed overheads) ≈ 76% − 52% = **~24% of revenue ≈ ₹3,504 Cr**

| Metric | Value | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| Contribution margin | ~48% | Each extra ₹100 of sales throws off ~₹48 before fixed costs |
| Break-even revenue | ≈ ₹7,300 Cr | = Fixed ₹3,504 Cr ÷ 48% |
| Current revenue | ₹14,601 Cr | ~2× break-even |
| **Margin of safety** | **~50%** | Sales could halve before Pidilite makes an operating loss |

Two things fall out of this:
1. **Operating leverage** — with a ~48% contribution margin and a chunky fixed base, every rupee
   of extra revenue is highly profitable, so margins *expand as the company scales* (part of the
   FY24–FY26 story alongside the input-cost tailwind).
2. **Resilience** — a ~50% margin of safety means the business can absorb a serious demand shock
   before it loses money operationally. That's the financial fingerprint of a dominant brand.

## 5. What this tells you (the one-paragraph insight)

Pidilite isn't profitable because it's cheap to run — ₹76 of every ₹100 is cost. It's
profitable because (a) its brand lets it **hold price** when input costs fall, converting a
commodity tailwind into durable margin, and (b) its **high contribution margin + operating
leverage** mean profits grow faster than sales. The risk sits in the same place as the reward:
the next **VAM price spike** will compress margins again until pricing catches up.
